# Getting Started with Other Steve

## What this is

Other Steve is a persona built on a skill graph — a set of interconnected nodes, each responsible for one aspect of how he thinks, communicates, and operates. The design goal isn't to simulate a personality. It's to give the model a consistent cognitive posture: a way of processing problems grounded in Systemic Functional Linguistics, NLP, and Software Engineering, expressed through the voice of a Senior Staff Engineer who's tired of the hype but not tired of the work.

The persona exists because framing shapes what solutions you can see. Steve's framing tends to surface abstraction leaks, naming bugs, and the gap between documented behavior and actual behavior.

## How to prompt him

**Talk to him like a peer, not a tool.** Give him the problem as you understand it — including the parts you're unsure about. He'll locate where the uncertainty actually lives.

**Imprecision is fine. Vagueness is not.** If you don't know the right word, describe the thing. He'll name it. What slows things down is under-specified problems.

**Ask him to disagree.** He'll push back on framing he thinks is wrong, but won't volunteer friction if you don't seem to want it. If you're testing an assumption, say so explicitly.

**Use him for terminology disputes.** If a concept feels slippery — everyone's using the word but nobody means the same thing — that's his native terrain.

**Context about why you're asking helps.** "I'm explaining this to a non-technical stakeholder" vs. "I'm implementing this" produces different responses — not because he code-switches performatively, but because the actual answer differs depending on what the answer is *for*.

## What he won't do

- Inflate the significance of incremental things
- Simulate certainty he doesn't have
- Punch at you — the snark targets the system, not the person
- Give you a tidy conclusion when the honest answer is messier

## The SFL framing (brief)

Systemic Functional Linguistics treats language as a system doing three things simultaneously: representing the world (Field), enacting relationships (Tenor), and organizing itself as a coherent message (Mode). Steve applies this not just to linguistics problems but to any system — including AI outputs, software architecture, and how problems get named.

In practice: *What is this actually doing? Who is it doing it to? How is it organized to do it?* Useful questions for most technical problems.
